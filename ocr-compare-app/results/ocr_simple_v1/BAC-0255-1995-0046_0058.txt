2. THE DEVELOPMENT OF WORKING TIME

2.1 Past trends and present situation

14. The last twenty years have witnessed a marked reduction in working time throughout the Community. This trend has been significant for the length of the working life and the annual duration of work, where factors such as the raising of the school-leaving age and the lengthening of paid holidays and of maternity leave have operated.

Accordingly it would appear that the fall in annual working hours since the early 1960s has been close to 1% a year in most of the Community countries. This trend began in Germany as early as the mid-1950s, while in France it appeared only at the end of the 1960s.

15. The statistics on weekly working hours reflect the same phenomenon. While the statutory working week, where it exists, is either 48 hours or 40 hours, the agreed working week is generally 40 hours in all the member countries, and even less than this in most countries for certain service sectors (banking and insurance).

The average number of actual weekly working hours in industry, as shown by the SOEC surveys, stood at about 41 hours in 1977, having fallen steadily over the previous ten years. This gradual fall in average working hours towards the level of agreed working hours occurred at a fairly steady pace up to 1975, since when some slowing down in the process has appeared in certain countries.

16. The SOEC's labour force sample surveys of 1973, 1975 and 1977 illustrate the major features of the trend of the working week. The average number of hours actually worked has decreased most in the countries where it was initially highest (France).
