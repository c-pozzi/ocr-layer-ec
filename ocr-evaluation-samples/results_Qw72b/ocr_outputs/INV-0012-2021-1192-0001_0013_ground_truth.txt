        This is nonetheless a small crumb of comfort for the Ad-
ministration (the AFL-CIO has in any case announced its intention
to pursue the matter in the Supreme Court). The 'real wage insurance'
plan, the centrepiece of the anti-inflation strategy now appears to
be in considerable trouble on Capitol Hill. Under the plan, workers
who settled within the 7% guidelines would be insured against in-
flation, the government making up the difference between what they
had received as an increase and any increase of the inflation rate
over 7%. Initial reaction from Congress was reasonably favourable
if uncommitted but there now appears to be little enthusiasm for
the plan. For a start, the AFL-CIO would not support it (having
rejected the voluntary package as a whole) and secondly as infla-
tion was increasing so too were the likely costs of the scheme. It
now therefore seems extremely unlikely that Congress will pass the
necessary legislation. Meanwhile labour unions are arguing the
case for compensation for inflation. (i.e. real wage insurance
paid by employers rather than government) in current wage negotia-
tions. Not only has the Administration probably failed to lay a vital
foundation stone for its anti-inflation strategy, but it has inad-
vertently established a principle for inflationary settlements.
